# Composites & Body Manufacturing

## Why Composites?
A solar car is energy-limited. Every gram costs watt-hours. Metal body was ruled out immediately.
We built a **non-structural composite body over the spaceframe** — a deliberate choice for 
manufacturing simplicity given the vehicle's size.

## Material Selection

**Fibre: E-glass (200gsm twill)**  
Carbon fibre would have been ideal but at 5–10× the cost with no autoclave available, it was 
never realistic. For a non-structural body that just needs to hold its aerodynamic shape, 
E-glass is the right call.

**Resin: Epoxy with amine-based hardener (slow pot life)**  
Chosen over polyester for:
- Lower shrinkage: ~1–2% vs ~4–8% for polyester
- Better fibre adhesion
- Superior dimensional stability (critical for holding aero contour)  

Slow hardener = 30–45 min pot life. On large panels, fast hardener causes dry spots 
and voids before wet-out is complete.

**Core: Soric XF (polyester foam)**  
Sandwich construction: 2× 200gsm E-glass on either face of Soric XF core.  
Honeycomb core rejected — requires vacuum bagging or autoclave for proper consolidation.

## The Mould — A Cost-Constrained Innovation
Standard route: CNC-machined polyurethane foam mould. Too expensive, CNC beds hard to source in Delhi.

Our approach (inspired by boat building):
1. Laser-cut 2mm mild steel sheets as cross-sectional body profiles
2. Welded into a box frame
3. Continuous steel skin sheet welded over them as layup surface

**Result: ~1/5th the cost of the conventional foam mould route.**

Challenges encountered and solved:
- **Thermal distortion** from welding the skin sheet → worked around during layup
- **Mould flex** on 5m steel sheet → solved by welding steel angle sections along edges as a backbone

## Layup & Finishing
Hand layup process. Male mould = rough outer surface, sanded and filled post-cure.  
Upper fairing covered by solar panels — finish was a low priority relative to structural integrity.
